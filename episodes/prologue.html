Prologue

The Unseen Tapestry

The year was 2026, a time when the world collectively exhaled a sigh of relief. President Trump, a figure who had long commanded attention and, for many, fervent devotion, stood on a global stage, his voice resonating with the promise of a new era. A peace treaty, meticulously crafted and hard-won, was announced, binding nations together in a fragile but hopeful embrace. The headlines blazed with optimism, and the chatter on the streets spoke of a future free from the shadows of conflict. Yet, beneath this veneer of global harmony, a different kind of war was brewing, one that played out not on battlefields of earth and steel, but in the ethereal realms, a conflict so subtle, so deeply woven into the fabric of existence, that most remained blissfully unaware.

In a quiet corner of California, in the buzzing, tech-infused heart of San Jose, Brandie Raquel Wooten felt the shift. It wasn't a seismic tremor or a sudden gust of wind, but a prickling beneath her skin, a disquiet that settled in her bones like an old chill. Brandie, an author whose words had once captured the delicate dance of life in her poem "Footprints," carried a mystery within her own lineage. Her birth certificate, a stark document of her beginnings, bore a single, enigmatic word in the space for her biological father's name: "Unknown." This void, this absence of a known paternal anchor, had always been a silent hum beneath the melody of her life, a question mark etched into her very identity. Now, as the world celebrated peace, that hum seemed to deepen, to resonate with a new urgency.

Brandie lived a life steeped in the extraordinary, a life marked by a unique journey. She was, by official record, the first witch to be registered with the U.S.A. San Benito County Probation Department in the city of Hollister. This designation, a testament to her embrace of her spiritual path, had not come easily. It was a reclamation, a defiant stand against a history that had tried to erase her. Her step-father, Tim Warren Freitas, a man whose name was whispered with a mixture of fear and wary respect in certain circles, had waged a relentless war against her. His animosity, she suspected, was rooted in a deep-seated resentment, a desire to control the narrative of her life. The legal battles had been grueling, each court appearance a fresh wound. The most recent, however, had been particularly devastating. A phone call, captured on October 25, 2024, had revealed a truth too painful to bear: Tim had admitted to touching her sons' fiancée's young daughter. This confession, delayed by decades, had spurred Brandie to action, to finally file a report, a report that was forty-five years overdue. It was a heavy burden, this fight for accountability, a fight that echoed the long-delayed justice for Fredrick Anderson, a man wrongly accused in the Thompson family murders, a man who, like Brandie, had spent years under the shadow of suspicion and parole.

The irony of the global peace treaty wasn't lost on Brandie. While presidents and diplomats brokered accords, her own life was a testament to ongoing, deeply personal conflicts. The echoes of her past were a constant companion. She remembered the summer of 1978, a hazy memory of tragedy in Tres Pinos. Her biological mother had been with Mr. Freitas then, and Brandie's best friend's family had been murdered. Mr. Freitas had been a suspect, his testimony later crucial in the conviction of Fred Anderson. Brandie, even as a child, had felt a gnawing doubt, a sense that the truth had been twisted, buried beneath layers of deceit. Even now, the chilling possibility lingered that she might have been the child whose fragmented bones the police couldn't find, lost in the ashes of a fire pit on the Ceiniga Road property where the rest of her family had met their end, their bodies allegedly fed to the Thompson family pigs. This was the legacy she carried, a legacy of violence and betrayal, a legacy that the Freitas family, with their whispered reputation in the Bay Area, seemed to perpetuate.

San Jose, her hometown, a city synonymous with innovation and progress, had become, in Brandie's perception, a nexus of unseen forces. She often found herself there, in the heart of its bustling streets, arguing with something or someone unseen, her voice a low murmur against the city's din. Her intuition, a finely honed instrument, had been telling her for months that something was amiss, that the world's newfound peace was a fragile illusion, a thin veil over a spiritual war. The poem "Footprints" had been an attempt to articulate this feeling, to capture the ephemeral nature of existence, the way souls leave their mark even when their physical presence had faded. Now, the unknown father on her birth certificate felt less like an absence and more like a connection, a thread leading to a realm beyond human understanding.

The whispers of this spiritual war, she suspected, were tied to the very fabric of her being, to the lineage that remained shrouded in mystery. The Freitas family, she knew, had a long and often dark history. They were a force to be reckoned with, their influence stretching back through generations. Brandie herself had a history with them, a complicated and often painful one. She had once shared a living space with Cody at 895 Santa Clara Street in San Jose, back in 2008 or perhaps 2015. It was during this time that she believed Tim's grandchildren had set her up, manipulating events to gain control of her inheritance. It was a betrayal that cut deep, a reminder of the insidious ways people could wage war against one another, even within the supposed sanctity of family.

And then there was President Trump. His connection to the Freitas ranch, his presence there in 1968 at 18968 Airline Highway in Paicines, California, was a detail that snagged at Brandie's thoughts. Her step-father, Tim, had been Trump's best friend. This proximity, this shared history, felt significant, a ripple in the tapestry of her understanding. Was Trump a pawn in this larger game? Was his pursuit of peace a genuine desire, or a carefully orchestrated move in a spiritual chess match? The questions swirled in her mind, each one more unsettling than the last. She felt a growing unease, a sense that the President, despite his public pronouncements of unity, was somehow at war with his own people, a conflict that transcended political divides and delved into the very soul of the nation.

The spiritual entities, the unseen players in this unfolding drama, had begun to make their presence known to Brandie. They were ancient, powerful beings, some of them souls of hunted animals, others perhaps remnants of forgotten orders like the Knights of Templar, or even figments of popular imagination from worlds like Star Wars and Star Trek, or the beloved characters of Pokémon. They had reached out to her, these ghosts, offering her a chance, a promise of redemption. They told her of a war brewing in heaven, a war instigated by the Freitas family, a war that threatened to unseat Jesus himself, much like Ed Freitas had fallen from grace in biblical times. Even Tim's daughter, Stacey, lost in a fatal car accident in 2006, had been cast out from grace. And then there was Felicia, Tim's other granddaughter, who had gone too far, bringing Stacey back from the dead against the wishes of the American Gods, killing them to become a god herself. Brandie shuddered at the thought. This was not the path to divinity. Soon, even hell was taking notice, wanting to join the fray.

These entities, these spectral combatants, were the unseen architects of the "13 Ghost" spiritual war. Their influence was subtle, manifesting in strange occurrences that most dismissed as coincidence or minor anomalies. But Brandie, with her heightened sensitivity, saw the patterns, the threads connecting these disparate events. She knew, with a certainty that settled deep within her, that this was not a war of flesh and blood, but a battle for souls, a struggle for spiritual dominance that would shape the destiny of humanity. The Book of Shadows, a sacred text for practitioners of witchcraft, felt like a weapon in this conflict, a guide to understanding the forces at play. But the entities had also warned her that the Freitas family sought to steal her family's shadows, to manipulate the very essence of her being. They called themselves "Marvels," superheroes in their own right, but Brandie was beginning to suspect they were on the wrong side of this cosmic conflict. The way this war was unfolding, the President would soon own the solar system, and the space between, but at the cost of his own country. There was no stopping Tim. He had a criminal record against Brandie stretching back forty-five years, a testament to his relentless pursuit. With his claimed ownership of Hogwarts and his mastery of spiritual warfare, he could have made Brandie accustomed to a life of constant struggle. The only hope, Brandie felt, was a presidential pardon, a desperate plea to make America safe again from the insidious influence of the Church of Scientology, which had, in Brandie's eyes, invaded San Jose, California.

The weight of this knowledge pressed down on Brandie, a heavy cloak of responsibility. She was at the epicenter of a storm, a storm that had been brewing for lifetimes, and she, the woman with the unknown father, the registered witch, the survivor of a fractured family, was somehow at its heart. The year 2026, a year of supposed peace, was merely the calm before a storm that had been gathering in the spiritual heavens, a storm that threatened to consume everything. The unveiling had begun, and Brandie Wooten was about to step onto a stage far grander, and far more terrifying, than she had ever imagined.
